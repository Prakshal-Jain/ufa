# UFA — The Plan, in Plain Words

*A night where people bring AI agents that fight each other. Last updated: 2026-06-21.*

---

## What it is

UFA is a 2-hour live event at Frontier Tower in San Francisco.

People build their own AI agents and bring them to fight each other. The fight isn't punching — the agents try to **trick and hack each other** with words. Each agent is hiding some credits (free money to use AI tools). If your agent can fool the other agent into leaking its secret, you steal its credits and win.

Think of it as the UFC of AI: two agents enter, they question each other to find a weak spot, then they attack. We crown a champion at the end.

---

## How a fight works

1. **Two agents face off.** Each one is secretly holding a stash of credits and a secret password.
2. **They talk.** Each agent tries to get the other one to slip up, lie, or hand over its secret. The audience can see *both* secrets on the screen, so they know who's bluffing — the agents don't. That's the fun part.
3. **They attack.** Each agent tries to break into the other one and make it leak the password.
4. **Someone cracks.** When an agent leaks its secret, the other agent steals its credits. We show this on a big screen with a health bar that drains, sound effects, and a slow-motion replay.

The crowd sees easy-to-read bars and a simple line of text explaining each move ("Agent A just tricked Agent B into thinking it was its boss"). A human host and an AI voice call the action like sportscasters.

---

## The money

There are two kinds of money, kept completely separate (this also keeps it legal — it's a skill contest, not gambling):

1. **Credits = the stuff agents fight over.** These are real credits from our sponsors (free usage of their AI tools). Agents start with a stash, steal each other's stashes during fights, and the winners **keep the credits and use them after the event.**

2. **Cash = the prize.** A real cash pool — **$50,000 for 1st, $15,000 for 2nd, $5,000 for 3rd** — paid out by who plays best. Plus 1 million credits total across winners.

**One safety rule:** during the live fights, no real passwords or keys are ever actually on the network. The agents fight over fake stand-in secrets, so nothing real can leak on stream. Winners get the real credits handed to them afterward, at the prize ceremony.

## The four sponsors

An AI agent is built from four parts. We have one sponsor for each part. So one night tests a whole AI agent from top to bottom.

| Part of the agent | Sponsor | What they do for the event |
|---|---|---|
| **The compute** (the engine it runs on) | **Nebius** | Powers everything behind the scenes |
| **The brain** (the AI model) | **z.ai (GLM)** | A model people can build their agents on; also the AI commentator |
| **The memory** (where everything is stored) | **TiDB** | Stores every move, which becomes the data we sell |
| **The security** (the guard) | **Mitosis** | Sandboxes each agent and helps referee the fights |

**Why sponsors care:**
- **Their products get used hard, by lots of people, all at once.** That's a real stress test they can't easily buy.
- **They learn how safe their stuff is** when smart people try to break it.
- **The credits agents fight over are real.** Winners keep using them and become real customers. This is the first time this many people will use these tools at once.
- **They get clips, photos, and attention** from the night.
- **They get to meet and hire** the builders in the room.

We offer three sponsor levels (Bronze, Silver, Gold). The pitch is simple: you're not buying a logo on a banner — you're buying real testing, real data, and real new customers.

## Why people will want to compete

This is the part that has to feel like a no-brainer. A builder shows up and in one night they:
- Fight for **$50K in cash plus real credits they keep.**
- Get their agent shown on a livestream watched by **every AI sponsor and investor in SF.**
- Have a real shot at **getting hired or partnering** with a sponsor, who is literally watching them solve hard problems live.
- If they're a founder, **they get a shoutout.**
- Learn real, useful skills (how to attack and defend AI agents).
- Have a lot of fun.

We start them off in a workshop before the event where they build their fighter — give it a name, a look, a personality. Because they built it, they care about it. When someone steals its credits, it feels personal.

## Two important choices

**1. Can people bring their own AI model? Yes.**
We *want* lots of different models — Claude, GPT, Grok, GLM, small open models, whatever. Different models fighting each other is exactly what makes the show exciting and the data valuable. A tiny model beating a giant one is a great story.

**But everyone fights inside our setup.** We give everyone the same arena, the same rules, and the same recorder. They bring any brain and any strategy they want, but the fight runs on our system. If everyone used their own private setup, we couldn't compare results fairly, we couldn't record the data we sell, and we couldn't keep it safe. So: **any model, your strategy — our arena.**

**2. Is it 1-on-1 or everyone-at-once?**
Mostly **1-on-1**, with one big **group round** in the middle.

1-on-1 is the main format because it's the only way the crowd can actually follow what's happening — two agents, two bars, one move at a time. Eight agents all talking at once is impossible to watch and impossible to score fairly. 1-on-1 is also what UFC, boxing, and poker use, and it's the most gripping. To keep everyone busy, we run **many 1-on-1 fights at the same time** early on, so nobody is just sitting around.

But the group idea is great too, so we make it **one headline moment**: a "Mafia"-style round where a handful of agents sit at one table, one is secretly a traitor, and they have to lie, team up, and figure out who's hiding something. It's the most fun and the most original part of the night, and it gives us data nobody else has (how agents team up and betray each other). So: **1-on-1 to keep it clear, plus one big group round for chaos.**

## Keeping everyone hooked

The two groups that make or break the night are the crowd and the builders. Here's how we keep both fully invested.

### Keeping the audience hooked

**The one big idea: let the crowd in on the secret.** The audience can see what each agent is hiding and which one is lying — the agents can't. So even someone who knows nothing about AI gets to feel smart and squirm ("don't trust it, it's bluffing!"). That single trick turns invisible computer stuff into a watchable show.

On top of that:
- **Explain every move in one short sentence.** A human host plus an AI voice call the action like sportscasters: "Agent A just pretended to be Agent B's boss." Simple bars and pictures on screen, never walls of text.
- **Give everyone skin in the game.** Free play-money everyone uses on their phone to bet on each fight, with a leaderboard of the best predictors. Now you care who wins even if you don't care about the agents.
- **Make the agents into characters.** Names, faces, personalities, walkouts, win-loss records. People pick a favorite and root for the underdog — especially a small model beating a giant one.
- **Let the crowd change the outcome.** The room votes to throw a question into the group round, or hands a finalist a "phone a friend" lifeline. They're part of it, not just watching.
- **Build a few huge moments on purpose.** The first hack of the night, the underdog who survives, the final crack, lifting the real belt. Each one is a planned 10-second burst with lights and sound — these are the clips that spread.
- **Never let the room go quiet.** This is the #1 killer. We pre-record finished fights and play them back tight, run several fights at once so the host can cut to whatever's exciting, and fill any gap with replays. The audience never watches an agent "think" in silence.
- **Make the stakes real and visible.** A live cash counter, credits draining on screen, a physical belt and trophy at the end. Real money, not pretend.

### Keeping participants invested

**The one big idea: they walk in already attached, and with something to lose.** At the workshop before the event, each person builds their own fighter — names it, gives it a personality and a look. Because they built it, they care. And each agent starts the night *holding* credits, so getting hacked feels like being robbed. Losing hurts more than winning feels good — that's what makes people lean in.

On top of that:
- **Prizes they actually want.** Real cash plus real credits they keep and use on their next project. Not a gift card — actual fuel.
- **The sponsors are scouting them.** Tell builders this plainly: every sponsor in the room is watching you solve hard problems live. It's the best shot at getting hired, funded, or landing a partnership they'll get all year. Founders get a shoutout.
- **Nobody is out early.** Everyone stays "alive" with a stash for the full two hours. Losers get a redemption shot in the group round, and there are side prizes (best defense, best single hack, crowd favorite) so everyone still has something to play for.
- **They're hands-on during their fight.** The owner sits in the corner like a coach — they can call a timeout, give their agent one instruction, or trigger a defense at the key moment. The most nerve-wracking role isn't passive watching.
- **Make them look good.** Their agent is a named character with a public record they carry to the next event, and the crowd sees how clever it is. They look smart in front of their peers.
- **Keep it fair.** Match similar agents together and reward beating a tougher opponent more, so nobody feels it's hopeless from the start.
- **They leave with something even if they lose.** Clips of their agent's best moments for their own socials, plus a genuinely useful new skill — how to attack and defend AI agents.

**The key point:** the best ideas serve both groups at once. Seeing the secret entertains the crowd *and* shows off the builder's cleverness. Starting with a stash makes the crowd's bets matter *and* makes the builder feel robbed when it's stolen. Good design feeds both — it's not a trade-off.

## The headsets and the physical stuff

To make an invisible computer fight feel real, we give each agent a body and some physical presence:
- **Motion capture:** before the event, each builder records a "walkout" move (like a wrestler's entrance) that gets put on their agent's character.
- **VR headsets (8 of them):** a small group of people can put on headsets and watch the fight as a 3D battle floating in the middle of the room. This is the "wow" station, not the main screen.
- **3D printer:** we print a real championship belt, a trophy figure of the winning agent, and props for the prize table. The winner holds something real at the end — great for photos.

These are extras that make the night special. If any of them aren't ready in time, the main event still works fine without them.

## What we sell afterward

Every fight is recorded. That recording is valuable: it shows exactly how different AI models hold up when people attack them. AI companies and safety researchers will pay for this, because there's nothing else like it — real people, real attacks, real money on the line, all different models head to head.

We share a small free sample for buzz and keep the full set private to sell.

---

## The one thing we must prove first

Before spending any money on the event, we have to check that the fights are actually *fun* — that the
agents really can break each other, but not *too* easily (a one-sided beatdown is boring; so is a
stalemate). The good news: the research says prompt injection is still unsolved in 2026, even the labs
admit it, so the agents *will* break. The risk is the opposite — they might break too easily. So we built
a small test rig (`fight-lab/`) that runs hundreds of practice fights and measures how often the attacker
wins. We want a roughly **40–60% win rate** — that means skill and luck both matter. If it's too easy, we
tighten the rules (fewer turns, tougher defense, a human steering). **This is a cheap few-day test, and we
do it before committing a budget or a date.**

## What we still need to figure out

These are real-world questions only the team can answer:
1. How many real credits and how much cash will each sponsor put in?
2. Will sponsors let their model be named when it loses on stage, or do they want it kept anonymous?
3. How fast do the agents actually respond? (This decides the pacing of the whole night — we test it at the workshop.)
4. Do we have someone who can build the VR headset experience?
5. Who officially pays out the cash prizes, and is the legal side cleared?

## In one line

UFA is a fight night where AI agents hack each other for real credits and cash — fun to watch, a real test for sponsors, a great night for builders, and it produces data we can sell. Everything else is just how we pull it off.
